Water in Your Food — Virtual Water & Food Footprints

Most of the water you "use" is hidden inside the food you eat. This page explains how food production consumes water, why different foods vary so much, and practical ways you can reduce your food-related water footprint.

What is "Virtual Water"?

Virtual water is the freshwater used to produce the food and products we consume — from growing crops to processing, packaging and transport. When you eat a meal, you are also consuming the water that went into producing it. The water footprint of a food item counts all water used throughout its life cycle.

Types of water in footprints — three commonly used categories:

  • Green water: rainwater stored in soil used directly by plants (important for crops).
  • Blue water: surface and groundwater used for irrigation and processing.
  • Grey water: the freshwater required to dilute pollutants and return water to acceptable quality after production.

Food & Water — Quick Visuals

Coffee & Tea

Coffee and tea have relatively high virtual water because coffee beans and tea leaves are water-intensive crops (and coffee requires processing). A single cup of coffee can represent roughly 100–200 liters of water (growing + processing). Instant coffee and espresso have different footprints, but the key idea is: many small daily cups add up.

Meat & Animal Products

Animal-based foods tend to have the largest water footprints per kilogram. This is because animals are fed crops (which require water), and raising livestock uses water for drinking, cleaning, and processing. Example averages:

  • Beef: ~10,000–15,400 L/kg (varies by farming system and feed)
  • Pork: ~4,000–6,000 L/kg
  • Chicken: ~2,000–4,300 L/kg
  • Egg: ~200 L / egg (approx.)

Dairy (Milk, Cheese, Yogurt)

Dairy products have significant footprints due to feed and processing. For example, one liter of milk might be around 200–1000 L (values depend a lot on production system). Cheese is water-intensive because it takes many liters of milk to make 1 kg of cheese: typical footprints are in the thousands of liters/kg.

Cereals, Vegetables & Legumes

Plant-based foods generally require less blue water per calorie than meat. Examples: rice ~2,500 L/kg, wheat ~1,300 L/kg, potatoes ~290 L/kg. Legumes and pulses often have lower footprints and are protein-efficient alternatives to meat.

How to Reduce Food-Related Water Use

Eat more plant-based meals

Shifting some meals away from meat and dairy to legumes and vegetables reduces your virtual water substantially.

Reduce food waste

Nearly one third of food produced is wasted — and so is the water used to produce it. Plan meals & store food properly.

Choose sustainable products

Look for locally grown, seasonal, and sustainably produced items (less irrigation, responsible farming).

Smarter coffee & nuts choices

Enjoy coffee in moderation; choose blends/brands that publish sustainable sourcing. Nuts (like almonds) are water-intensive — eat sparingly.

Sources & notes: Approximate values are illustrative and vary across sources and regions. For rigorous research and country-level numbers, consult publications such as the Water Footprint Network and peer-reviewed LCA studies.
